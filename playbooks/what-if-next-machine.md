# The What If Next Machine

## Methodology and Application Playbook



*This is the canonical practitioner reference for the What If Next Machine. Read it once before running your first File. Return to Part Four whenever a quantitative step needs checking against a worked number.*



## Part One: The model, the Machine, and the File

Three names carry three distinct jobs. Keeping them separate is the first discipline.

**What If Next** is the model. It is a way of seeing a market. The claim underneath it is that the hidden signal in any industry is not the technology everyone is discussing. It is the constraint that technology is exposing, the one binding limitation the whole system is organizing around whether anyone has named it or not.

**The What If Next Machine**, or the Machine, is the engine. It converts a trigger, a deal, a thesis, a provocative public claim, into a structured, falsifiable, numerically grounded view of where a market is headed and why.

**The What If File**, or a File, is the output. It is a living dossier, not a one-time essay. The thesis at the top holds across many cycles. The branch weights, the signal ledger, and the kill conditions move every cycle as evidence arrives. A File is maintained the way a position is maintained, not published the way an article is.

The distinction matters in plain speech. "The What If Next view of enterprise AI" describes a way of seeing. "I ran the Aisera acquisition through the Machine" describes an act. "File No. 9 is due for its quarterly pass" describes an artifact under maintenance. Confusing the three is how a single computed weight gets treated as a permanent truth rather than the current state of a living estimate.



## Part Two: The theory underneath

### 2.1 Why constraints, not trends

Most market analysis follows visible motion: funding rounds, product launches, executive claims, adoption curves, the language a category adopts. Visible motion shows that something is happening. It rarely explains why the system is under pressure in that particular place and not somewhere else.

A market does not reorganize because everything about it is weak. It reorganizes because one or two constraints dominate the system at a given time. This is the Theory of Constraints applied to market structure: relieve anything other than the binding constraint and the system looks better in the spot you touched while the whole barely moves.

The Machine's job is to find the binding constraint before it becomes obvious, to test who owns it and how, and to map how power relocates when it eventually relieves. The branches in every File represent the plausible ways that relocation plays out.

### 2.2 The constraint taxonomy

Naming the type of constraint is how you answer when, not only where.

| Type | What binds | Relief mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Technical | A capability does not yet work at the needed quality | A capability ships at the needed quality |
| Economic | Unit cost does not clear the buyer's payback threshold | Unit cost crosses the threshold |
| Regulatory | A ruling, statute, or enforcement posture forecloses the move | A ruling or enforcement action lands |
| Behavioral | Trust has not accrued for the buyer to act | Trust accrues through repeated safe use |
| Financial | The buyer cannot fund the change inside the current balance sheet | Financing structure changes, or the ask shrinks |
| Operational | The surrounding process cannot absorb the capability without re-engineering | The process gets re-engineered around the capability |
| Cultural | The organization's identity or incentives resist the change | Incentives realign, or a champion forces the issue |
| Architectural | A required layer or standard does not yet exist | The missing layer or standard gets built |

Constraints blend. A File names the dominant type and notes any secondary type with its own clock. An antitrust posture sits as a regulatory constraint riding on top of an otherwise economic one. The blend, not just the primary type, determines the realistic timeline.

### 2.3 The type-to-clock table

Where without when is the difference between being early and being right.

| Type | Clock | Character |
|---|---|---|
| Technical | Engineering | Fast once feasible |
| Economic | Cost-curve | Predictable, watch the curve |
| Regulatory | Litigation and rulemaking | Slow and lumpy |
| Behavioral | Adoption-curve | Gradual, then sudden |
| Financial | Budget-cycle | Stepwise, tied to fiscal calendars |
| Operational | Process-redesign | Slow, organization-dependent |
| Cultural | Incentive-realignment | Slowest, resists forcing |
| Architectural | Standards-emergence | Lumpy, then fast once a standard tips |


## Part Three: Detection, ownership, and the moat

### 3.1 The six detection tells

Run these as symptom-driven detectors before touching any structural model. They find constraints by reading what the market is already confessing.

**Spend without return.** Money flows in and measurable ROI does not come back out. The capital is paying to push against a wall.

**Where pilots die.** Initiatives reach proof of concept and stall at the same production gate in every company trying. The thing that kills them at that consistent point is the constraint.

**The universal workaround.** Everyone independently builds the same patch for the same gap. The patch marks the missing layer.

**The preemptive apology.** Every vendor apologizes for the same limitation before being asked. They are all bounded by the same wall.

**The not-yet chorus.** The people closest to the work keep saying the capability is not ready for the real thing. Listen to what specifically is not yet ready, not that it is not ready.

**Convergence.** Competitors independently reposition onto the same layer and start using the same word for what they are doing. That language convergence names the constraint. They are all circling the same wall.

### 3.2 The force scan

Run Porter's five forces plus complementors as a six-location coverage scan: buyer power, supplier power, new entrants, substitutes, rivalry, and complements. The sixth, complementors, is where AI-era constraints disproportionately hide, since a complementor's pricing or access decision can bind a market Porter's original five would read as healthy.

The discipline between the two methods is strict. Porter lists, the Machine ranks. The force scan guarantees coverage so nothing is missed. The tells and the constraint logic decide which location is binding right now.

### 3.3 Ownership and the owner's game

List who owns each layer of the value chain, then mark the binding layer. Watch for the case where no vendor owns the binding constraint at all. Sometimes it sits inside the buyer's own risk function or balance sheet. When that happens, the prize goes to whoever relieves the buyer, not to whoever holds a supply-side layer.

Owners of a binding constraint have every incentive to keep it binding and extract rent from it. Name the move, because it predicts what the owner will fight hardest to protect.

| Owner move | What it does | What erodes it |
|---|---|---|
| Defend | Hardens the wall, raises switching cost | A substitute routes around the wall entirely |
| Toll | Charges rent for passage without removing the wall | Regulation caps the toll, or a free alternative undercuts it |
| Absorb | Acquires the source of relief before it scales | The acquired capability is reproduced independently |
| Commoditize | A platform tears the wall down to win the layer above it | Whoever commoditizes must win the next layer up, or the move is self-defeating |

A complete ownership map names, for each layer, who pays, who captures, and who is liable if the layer fails.

### 3.4 The six moat tests

Run all six against any moat under examination. A moat is a profile across these six dimensions, not a binary judgment.

| Test | The question |
|---|---|
| Constraint fit | Does the moat sit on the binding constraint, or on a feature nobody is paying for? |
| Transferability | Does the advantage transfer to adjacent decisions, or only inside one narrow workflow? |
| Economic conversion | Has the moat converted to revenue, margin, or share, or is it still a story? |
| Control point leverage | Does owning this point give leverage over the rest of the system, or is it isolated? |
| Adversarial response | What does a well-funded rival do in response, and how long does the moat survive it? |
| Learning compounding | Does the system get stronger with every deployment, customer, action, and feedback loop? |

For a fast read under time pressure, score on the two that matter most for the specific position being tested: the one a moat passes most decisively, and the one that can kill it. That pairing is usually enough to locate it.

### 3.5 The cascade

Relieving the binding constraint does not end the story. It relocates the constraint. The cascade traces that relocation forward two or three links, so a File anticipates where the fight moves next rather than declaring victory at the first resolution. Each link is a market reorganization and a movement of the control point. Map three links and the shape of the next several years becomes visible, not just the next quarter. In the most rigorous runs, the cascade is rendered as a structural causal model with named paths and, where data supports it, estimated path coefficients.


## Part Four: The quantitative spine

This is the part most often named in prose and not actually run. A File that says "branch weights reflect a Bayesian update" without showing the prior vector, the signal-by-signal tempering, and the worked posterior has not run the spine. It has gestured at it. Every step below is fully computed so the arithmetic is checkable.

### 4.1 What the belief layer actually is

Branch weights are the output of a **tempered, or power-likelihood, Bayesian update over a latent categorical hypothesis**. This is the precise name. What the Machine does within a cycle is multiply tempered likelihoods from signals bearing on a latent winning pattern, producing a single point on the branch simplex.

The Dirichlet distribution does real work, but at a specific step. It supplies the prior mean and prior strength going into each cycle, and it supplies conjugate carryover across cycles in the cross-cycle update at Section 4.9. Within a single cycle, the procedure is a per-source tempered update: raise each signal's likelihood to an exponent set by its source grade, multiply across signals, multiply by the prior, and normalize. The arithmetic is identical to any other robust-Bayes device; the label matters because it correctly describes what is being computed and what can be checked.

### 4.2 The prior and the pseudo-count vector

The prior over branches is the mean of a Dirichlet parameterized by a pseudo-count vector alpha, one entry per branch, summing to alpha-zero.

**Rule for setting the prior.** Assign pseudo-counts based on base-rate judgment before the File's own evidence is run, as if you knew the category but not this specific case. A flat prior (equal pseudo-counts) is the right starting point when you have no credible reason to favor one branch. Adjust away from flat only when the base-rate justification is explicit and could be challenged.

**Illustrative prior (three-branch case):**

| Branch | Pseudo-count α | Prior mean |
|---|---|---|
| Incumbent Absorbs | 4.0 | 40.0% |
| Insurgent Wins Category | 3.0 | 30.0% |
| Substrate Commoditizes (null) | 3.0 | 30.0% |
| α₀ | 10.0 | 100.0% |

Every File must include a residual or null branch, the world where none of the main theses play out as expected. It cannot be assigned zero weight. The null keeps the File honest by holding probability mass for unknown unknowns.

### 4.3 Source grading and the temper exponent

Every signal has a source grade, and the grade sets the exponent that tempers its influence on the posterior. This is the mechanism that prevents a vendor forecast from moving a File the same way a primary filing does.

| Grade | Definition | Exponent τ |
|---|---|---|
| A | Motive-clean primary: SEC filings, court records, primary data, direct disclosure | 1.0 |
| B | Motive-aware or credible secondary: analyst notes, trade press, vendor-adjacent commentary | 0.5 to 0.6 |
| C | Secondary pending verification, or projection: founder interviews about future plans, unverified claims | 0.15 to 0.3 |

A Grade A signal enters at full strength. A Grade C signal enters at roughly one-fifth strength. The analyst still records the Grade C signal and its raw likelihood, because a ledger that silently drops weak evidence is less auditable than one that discounts it explicitly.

### 4.4 Redundancy and the independence assumption

The product-over-signals form used in Section 4.5 assumes the signals are independent given the branch. State that assumption explicitly in every File that runs the spine, because the redundancy correction below is a patch for the case where it fails.

When two signals report the same underlying fact through two outlets, a wire story and a press release describing the identical announcement, the second signal is tempered one step further down from its grade-implied exponent. Without this correction, one fact dressed in two sources inflates the posterior as if the evidence arrived twice.

**Worked example.** Two Grade B signals (τ = 0.6), two branches X and Y at 50-50 prior, both signals favor X with raw likelihood 0.75 for X and 0.388 for Y. Both signals are reporting the same underlying fact.

Counted naively, each at τ = 0.6:

- Evidence weight X: 0.75^0.6 × 0.75^0.6 = 0.8415 × 0.8415 = 0.7081
- Evidence weight Y: 0.388^0.6 × 0.388^0.6 = 0.5667 × 0.5667 = 0.3212
- Posterior on X: 0.7081 / (0.7081 + 0.3212) = **68.8%**

Applying the redundancy correction, the second signal is stepped down to τ = 0.3:

- Evidence weight X: 0.75^0.6 × 0.75^0.3 = 0.8415 × 0.9173 = 0.7719
- Evidence weight Y: 0.388^0.6 × 0.388^0.3 = 0.5667 × 0.7529 = 0.4267
- Posterior on X: 0.7719 / (0.7719 + 0.4267) = **64.4%**

The correction recovers 4.4 percentage points of avoided double-counting. Across a ledger with several correlated pairs, the cumulative correction can move a leading branch by three to four points, enough to change which branch leads.

### 4.5 The signal ledger and the within-cycle update

Each signal in the ledger carries: an identifier, a one-line description, the source, the grade, the temper exponent τ, and a likelihood for every branch on a relative zero-to-one scale. The scale represents how consistent each piece of evidence is with each branch being the dominant pattern. Only the ratios across branches carry information, since the update normalizes at the end. These are analyst judgments written down where they can be challenged, not statistical probabilities.

The update formula: Posterior ∝ Prior × Π L(signal | branch)^τ

Multiply each branch's tempered likelihoods across all signals to get its evidence weight. Multiply each evidence weight by the prior. Normalize. The result is the posterior for that cycle.

**Full worked example, three branches, three signals:**

Prior (from Section 4.2 above), α₀ = 10.

Signal ledger:

| ID | Signal | Source | Grade | τ | Incumbent | Insurgent | Null |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| S1 | Incumbent posts realized revenue tied to contested feature | Primary filing | A | 1.0 | 0.80 | 0.30 | 0.25 |
| S2 | Vendor-adjacent analyst note on insurgent share gain | Analyst | B | 0.6 | 0.35 | 0.75 | 0.35 |
| S3 | Founder interview projecting category capture | Interview | C | 0.2 | 0.30 | 0.70 | 0.30 |

Tempered likelihoods (raw likelihood raised to τ):

| Signal | Incumbent | Insurgent | Null |
|---|---|---|---|
| S1 (τ=1.0) | 0.8000 | 0.3000 | 0.2500 |
| S2 (τ=0.6) | 0.5327 | 0.8415 | 0.5327 |
| S3 (τ=0.2) | 0.7860 | 0.9311 | 0.7860 |

Evidence weights (product across all signals):

| Branch | Calculation | Weight |
|---|---|---|
| Incumbent Absorbs | 0.8000 × 0.5327 × 0.7860 | 0.33493 |
| Insurgent Wins Category | 0.3000 × 0.8415 × 0.9311 | 0.23506 |
| Substrate Commoditizes (null) | 0.2500 × 0.5327 × 0.7860 | 0.10467 |

Posterior (prior × evidence weight, normalized):

| Branch | Prior | Evidence weight | Posterior | Move |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Incumbent Absorbs | 40.0% | 0.33493 | 56.8% | ×1.42 |
| Insurgent Wins Category | 30.0% | 0.23506 | 29.9% | ×1.00 |
| Substrate Commoditizes (null) | 30.0% | 0.10467 | 13.3% | ×0.44 |

Read the moves alongside the weights. The ledger moved conviction toward Incumbent Absorbs and away from the null, while leaving Insurgent Wins almost exactly where the prior had it. That flat ×1.00 move is informative: the evidence for the insurgent, S2 and S3, was real but offset by S1's stronger, higher-grade signal. A flat move is not a non-result. It is the ledger reporting that this cycle's evidence was a wash for that branch.

### 4.6 Bayes factors

The ratio of two branches' evidence weights, computed before the prior enters, is the Bayes factor. It states how decisively the evidence alone, independent of starting belief, favors one branch over another.

From the worked example:
- Incumbent Absorbs vs. null: 0.33493 / 0.10467 = **3.20 to 1**
- Insurgent Wins vs. null: 0.23506 / 0.10467 = **2.25 to 1**

Reporting Bayes factors alongside posteriors lets a reader separate "the evidence favors this" from "the prior already favored this and the evidence barely moved it." Read factors against the Kass-Raftery interpretive scale, not by feel:

| Bayes factor | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 1 to 3 | Barely worth a mention |
| 3 to 20 | Positive evidence |
| 20 to 150 | Strong evidence |
| Above 150 | Decisive evidence |

A factor of 3.2 to 1 is positive but not strong. A File should describe it as a tilt toward the leading branch, not a settled case. Precision in language here is how the spine stays honest.

### 4.7 The floor and renormalization

Every File carries a floor, conventionally 5 percent, below which no branch reports its bare computed value. The floor exists because a branch computing to 1 or 2 percent usually reflects the limits of the analyst's likelihood judgments, not genuine near-impossibility. Reporting false precision at the tail invites overconfidence exactly where the evidence is thinnest.

The procedure is a single simultaneous pass. Identify all branches that fall below the floor. Raise each to the floor value at once. Compute the total mass added across all floored branches. Remove that same total, proportionally by each surviving branch's share, from the branches that were above the floor. Renormalize once.

**Worked example.** Three branches, strong evidence for B.

| Branch | Raw posterior |
|---|---|
| A | 12.4% |
| B | 87.2% |
| C | 0.4% |

C falls below the 5% floor. Total added to reach the floor: 4.6 percentage points. That amount is removed proportionally from A and B (A has 12.4% of the above-floor mass, B has 87.6%), and the result is renormalized.

| Branch | After floor |
|---|---|
| A | 11.8% |
| B | 83.2% |
| C | 5.0% (floored) |
| Sum | 100.0% |

### 4.8 The conditional causal tier

The causal tier runs only when a realized outcome panel exists, meaning actual resolved outcomes across comparable past cases, not merely opinions about an unresolved current situation. When the panel does not qualify, the tier is explicitly held and noted as "held, panel insufficient," not silently omitted. The absence is informative.

The tier has four components in three categories. Understanding the category matters because confusing prediction for causal estimation is a common failure mode.

**Two causal estimators** (these estimate actual causal effects):

A causal forest estimates a conditional average treatment effect across comparable cases, for instance the differential effect of a deal structure on time-to-scale across resolved acquisitions. A Bayesian structural time series event study estimates a counterfactual trajectory around a single dated intervention, for instance what an adoption curve would have done absent a specific regulatory action.

**One attribution component** (this is prediction plus explanation, not causal estimation):

A gradient-boosted classifier predicts which branch a new case most resembles, with Shapley attributions decomposing the prediction into per-feature contributions. This tells you what features drove the prediction. It does not, by itself, tell you that those features caused the outcome. The distinction matters for how findings are reported.

**One regime-conditioning model**:

A hidden Markov model infers which latent market regime is currently active. Regime is latent, meaning unobservable directly, so the model produces a posterior distribution over regimes rather than a single assigned label. Condition on that full posterior when using regime to inform branch weights. Hard-assigning a single regime label discards exactly the uncertainty the model was built to carry.

When the panel is too small to run the causal forest or BSTS responsibly, conventionally fewer than eight to ten comparable resolved cases, present the available cases as a structured comparison table and use the attribution component qualitatively as a feature read rather than a trained model. A small honest comparison is worth more than a model run past its statistical license.

### 4.9 The cross-cycle update

Within a cycle, Sections 4.5 through 4.7 produce a posterior. Across cycles, that posterior has to become the next cycle's prior without undermining falsifiability. The rule that achieves this uses a forgetting factor on the existing pseudo-counts plus evidence-derived counts added fresh each cycle:

**α ← λ · α + m · ê**

Here α is the pseudo-count vector entering the cycle, ê is this cycle's normalized evidence weight vector (the evidence weight terms from Section 4.5, normalized to sum to one), m is the total count of new evidence-strength added this cycle (calibrated to the number and grade of new signals, typically in the range of 3 to 8 per quarterly cycle), and λ is the forgetting factor, conventionally 0.7 to 0.9 depending on how fast the market actually changes.

This rule has a finite steady state. At λ = 0.8 and m = 5, total pseudo-count strength converges to α₀ = m / (1 − λ) = 5 / 0.2 = **25**. Prior conviction becomes sticky at that level rather than growing without bound. A File fed steady contrary evidence continues to move every cycle by a predictable amount, rather than moving less and less as accumulated prior strength swamps new evidence.

**What to avoid.** Adding a multiple of the posterior itself to the pseudo-count vector each cycle inflates α₀ without bound and compounds old conviction on top of itself, making a long-lived File progressively harder to move by evidence. A File that is asymptotically unfalsifiable is not a living dossier. It is a defended position dressed as analysis.

### 4.10 The sensitivity sweep

Before publishing a reweight, perturb the analyst-supplied likelihoods in the ledger and recompute the posterior. The convention is to vary each likelihood by plus or minus ten to fifteen percentage points and observe whether the leading branch changes.

**Worked example.** Perturbing the S1 likelihood for Incumbent Absorbs from the base value of 0.80, while holding S2 and S3 constant:

| S1 likelihood for Incumbent | Posterior: Incumbent Absorbs |
|---|---|
| 0.70 (−10%) | 53.5% |
| 0.80 (base) | 56.8% |
| 0.90 (+10%) | 59.7% |

In this case the leading branch holds across the full perturbation range. The File can report a robust result. When a plausible perturbation flips the leading branch, that fragility is the most important finding in that cycle's update, not the headline number, and should be reported as the central result rather than buried.

### 4.11 Calibration

A File that issues posteriors is issuing probabilistic forecasts, and forecasts can be scored. When a branch resolves, meaning the underlying question becomes a recorded fact, score that File's final pre-resolution posterior against the outcome using a proper scoring rule, a Brier score or a log score.

Track this across the full book of Files, not File by File in isolation. A practitioner whose 70 percent calls resolve true roughly 70 percent of the time across the book is calibrated. One whose 70 percent calls resolve true 90 percent of the time is understating confidence. One whose 70 percent calls resolve true 40 percent of the time has a systematic bias somewhere in the likelihood judgments, most often in how aggressively low-grade signals are weighted. Neither overconfidence nor underconfidence is safe; both are correctable if the calibration record is kept.

## Part Five: A complete run, end to end

Carry the worked example through all eight stages so the parts connect into a single motion.

**Stage one: surface the signal.** The trigger is S1, a realized revenue disclosure from the incumbent. Run signal hygiene first: does this event change what the constraint is, who owns it, or when it moves? Yes on all three counts. It is signal, not motion.

**Stage two: detect the constraint.** Run the tells. Convergence fires: rivals are repositioning onto the same contested layer and adopting the incumbent's language. The force scan points to rivalry as the binding force. Name the constraint as economic: the question is whether the insurgent's unit economics can reach scale before the incumbent's distribution advantage closes the gap. Clock: cost-curve, predictable but contested.

**Stage three: test the moat.** Run the six tests against the insurgent. Learning compounding passes strongly. Economic conversion is now confirmed as a live question by S1's existence. Control point leverage is weak, since the incumbent's distribution still gates most of the addressable market.

**Stage four: map ownership.** The contested layer sits between the incumbent, who owns distribution, and the insurgent, who owns the technical lead. Neither owns the buyer's underlying budget constraint. That gap is where the rent route lives.

**Stage five: model the owner's game.** The incumbent's move is toll, charging for access to distribution rather than building the contested layer itself. That is precisely the move that generates the convergence tell: it signals the layer is monetizable, which is why competitors start copying the language.

**Stage six: run the quantitative spine.** The prior is set at 40-30-30 from Section 4.2. The ledger runs three signals across Sections 4.3 through 4.5. Posterior: Incumbent Absorbs 56.8 percent, Insurgent Wins Category 29.9 percent, null 13.3 percent. All branches clear the 5 percent floor. Bayes factors of 3.2 and 2.25 over the null, both positive but not strong on Kass-Raftery. The File reports a tilt toward Incumbent Absorbs. Run the sensitivity sweep from Section 4.10 before publishing: the leading branch holds across the perturbation range.

**Stage seven: map the cascade.** If Incumbent Absorbs continues to lead, the next link in the cascade is the insurgent's remaining independent customers, who become the next contested layer. The link after that is a standards question: whether the tolling move generates enough cross-industry friction to invite a neutral infrastructure response.

**Stage eight: translate to action.** The incumbent's move is to keep tolling rather than building, since toll is working and absorption is expensive under current conditions. The insurgent's move is to find the segment the incumbent's distribution structurally cannot serve and defend it as a beachhead. A capital allocator's move is to wait for the next cycle's reweight before committing, since the current Bayes factor is positive but not strong.


## Part Six: Governance

### 6.1 The Unguarded Flank

Every File identifies the open strategic position nobody is currently occupying. Three gates determine whether a candidate position is a genuine flank or a wishful gap.

**Structural block.** Is there a specific reason no current player can occupy this position, not merely that none has tried yet?

**Constraint, not feature.** Does the position sit on the binding constraint itself, rather than on a feature that is useful but not gating?

**Compounds before arrival.** Does waiting allow someone else's flywheel to make the position unreachable before a new entrant could claim it?

A candidate must pass all three gates to count. Passing two and failing the third is the most common error, and it is worse than a clean miss because it looks like a real finding.

### 6.2 The Governor and two-speed control

The constraint thesis at the top of a File moves slowly, only on a named kill condition. The branch weights and the signal ledger move quickly, every cycle, as evidence arrives. Running both at the same speed is how a File loses its discipline. Revising the thesis on every signal update chases noise. Freezing the branch weights because the thesis feels settled produces a document that already knows what it is going to say.

A kill condition is a pre-specified falsifying observation: if this specific thing happens, the thesis is wrong, not just the weights. Kill conditions are written at File creation, not invented after the fact.

### 6.3 Generation-verification separation

A number is never trusted because it was computed. It is trusted because it was checked by someone other than the person who computed it, or by the same person at a different time with fresh eyes. The signal ledger's likelihood judgments should be examinable by any reader and challengeable by any co-author. Opacity in inputs is the fastest path to a File that confirms its own prior rather than interrogating it.

### 6.4 The four-action maintenance classification

Every cycle, a File receives exactly one of four dispositions. Logging the disposition is not optional: the maintenance log is how a File proves it is living rather than dormant.

| Disposition | When it applies |
|---|---|
| Ledger note | New evidence arrived but did not move the posterior meaningfully |
| Reweight | New evidence moved the posterior enough to warrant a republished branch map |
| Thesis review | A kill condition fired, requiring the constraint thesis itself to be re-examined |
| Retirement | The constraint has fully resolved, or the thesis has been structurally falsified |

The sensitivity sweep and calibration log from Sections 4.10 and 4.11 are run before every reweight and recorded alongside the disposition.

## Part Seven: File anatomy

### 7.1 Required sections

A complete File contains, at minimum and in working order:

A constraint thesis with type and clock. A force scan ranked by binding force. An ownership map with the owner's-game rent route named. The six moat tests run against the load-bearing moat. A weighted branch map summing to 100 percent with the 5 percent floor applied as a single simultaneous pass. A dated signal ledger with grade, temper exponent, and per-branch likelihoods for every signal. The posterior, Bayes factors against the null, and the Kass-Raftery read stated in plain language. The conditional causal tier, either run or explicitly held with the reason stated. The cascade mapped at least two links forward. The Unguarded Flank tested against all three gates. Actor reads for the incumbent, the insurgent or challenger, a capital allocator, and a regulator or buyer. A maintenance log with the current cycle's four-action disposition. The sensitivity sweep result. Source-graded references.

A File that is missing any of these is incomplete, not just light. The absent section is usually the one that would have been most uncomfortable to write.

### 7.2 Actor reads

Every File concludes with a read of the situation from each major class of participant. The incumbent's read differs from the challenger's differs from the capital allocator's differs from the regulator's or buyer's. Writing all four forces a test of whether the branch weights hold up from multiple vantage points, not just from the analyst's preferred one. A branch that looks overwhelmingly dominant from one actor's position and nonsensical from another's is signaling something that the single-vantage analysis missed.

### 7.3 The cascade in the File

The cascade section is not a repeat of the branch map. The branch map states what the plausible futures are and how much weight the current evidence assigns to each. The cascade states what happens next inside the leading branch, how the constraint relocates, where the control point moves, and what the fight looks like in the subsequent round. If the branch map is the map, the cascade is the path on it.

## Part Eight: Writing discipline

### 8.1 Argument structure

Every File and every essay built from it is led by one argument structure, with at most one supporting structure. Choose the structure that matches the reader's decision, not the one that makes the material easiest to organize.

The eight structures are: concede-then-pivot, thesis-and-kill-signal, constraint-first, steelman-then-break, structural disqualification, cost-of-error asymmetry, pattern accumulation, and two-sided weighted brief. Concede-then-pivot is the right default in collaborative and co-creation contexts. Constraint-first leads whenever the constraint has not yet been named in the public discourse.

### 8.2 Prose conventions

Paragraphs of three to five sentences, roughly sixty to one hundred twenty words, one idea per paragraph. Vary sentence length deliberately: a short sentence after a long one carries emphasis. No anaphora stacking, no rhetorical question chains, no antithetical "not X but Y" constructions, no ordinal scaffolding in prose, no staccato single-line paragraphs, no bold run-in labels outside instrument sections and tables.

Uncertainty is carried in the branch weights and the Kass-Raftery read, not in adjectives.
